## 1. 🔗 DATA & INTEGRATION
### *The clean-data race is now the AI-readiness race — and the PMS is the control layer*

The week's most operationally significant stories converge on a single theme: fragmented data is the actual bottleneck. 
Minor Hotels is building a new global data and AI platform from the ground up — unlike competitors layering AI onto legacy systems — with full deployment targeted by end-2026, supported by Google Cloud, Salesforce, and OneTrust to break down data silos.
 
The platform's lead architect Ian Di Tullio framed it simply: "AI is becoming the front door to travel — and with it, control over demand is shifting."
 The build uses 
Google Cloud's BigQuery and Vertex AI as the interoperable data and intelligence layer, enabling Minor Hotels to unify guest data across brands, regions, and digital touchpoints — with AI agents eventually tethered to this single source of truth to reliably manage bookings, curate itineraries, and resolve complex guest requests in real time.


On the vendor side, 
Model Context Protocol (MCP) is emerging as a practical architectural response: it introduces a controlled interface connecting AI systems to hotel infrastructure — replacing fragmented integrations — allowing PMS, CRS, and CRM platforms to be accessed and acted on in real time without replacing existing systems.
 
Aven Hospitality (formerly Sabre Hospitality Solutions) is embedding MCP directly within its SynXis CRS and Booking Engine, launching an Early Access Programme for select hotel chains in Q2 2026.
 Meanwhile, 
BCG warns that many hotel companies operate with fragmented technology systems — separate PMS, POS, CRM, and loyalty platforms — and will need to integrate these and build a centralised data platform as a single source of truth before AI can deliver meaningful returns.


**So what:** If you're a mid-market operator still running siloed systems, Minor Hotels and Aven's moves define the new baseline. This is the year a clean data layer separates operators who can activate AI from those paying for shelfware. PMS vendors who can't demonstrate an open API roadmap and MCP compatibility are a liability in renewal conversations.

## 2. 🔍 AI VISIBILITY & DISCOVERY
### *Travelers use AI to dream; they still book with brands — but properties invisible to AI are already losing the funnel*

The week's headline consumer data comes from Expedia Group's freshly released **AI Trust Gap** report. 
While AI is quickly becoming a go-to tool for trip planning, travelers still overwhelmingly rely on trusted travel brands for booking. 68% prefer to book with a trusted travel brand over AI chatbots or agents, and 66% say they would not trust an AI assistant to buy or book anything on their behalf — with only 8% comfortable booking through an AI platform.
 
Expedia Group's Chief AI & Data Officer Xavi Amatriain was direct: "Travelers don't have a technology problem with AI. They have a trust problem."


But the flip side is equally important. 
Discovery is moving fast: travelers are increasingly using AI to explore destinations, build itineraries, and generate ideas, shifting early-stage demand away from traditional search. Hotels risk losing influence in the early stages of trip planning if they are not represented or surfaced in AI-driven discovery environments.
 Lighthouse has responded with a concrete product: 
Lighthouse launched The Hotels Network app inside ChatGPT, powered by its Connect AI engine built on MCP, giving hotels a live-data bridge for brand-verified content, real rates, and direct-booking links — positioned explicitly as "a new distribution channel."
 
Cendyn has also launched AI Connect, pushing hotel Availability, Rates, and Inventory (ARI) directly into AI search platforms such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini through MCP — noting that before now, most of this information was surfaced to AI tools from OTAs.


**So what:** The discovery/booking split is the strategic crux of 2026. Properties that aren't MCP-connected are invisible at the top of the funnel while OTAs build their AI-native distribution relationships. The Trust Gap is real, but it's a temporary feature of early adoption — the window to get inventory into AI discovery channels is open now, not later.

## 3. 🏢 VENDOR MOVES
### *Genuine MCP launches vs. agentic relabelling — the field is splitting fast*


Deliverect, the restaurant technology platform serving more than 95,000 locations across 78 countries, launched Deliverect AI on April 9: a digital workforce of autonomous agents and smart assistants that continuously rewrite digital restaurant menus to increase revenue, resolve technical issues, and replace labour-intensive manual tasks that historically took weeks and tens of thousands in agency costs.
 Critically, 
prior to the announcement, a Deliverect AI agent autonomously designed, deployed, and optimised a marketing promotion for KFC that produced a 118% increase in sales, with no human involvement at any stage.
 This is a verified outcome — not a press release.

On the hotel side, 
PAR Technology and Square both unveiled AI agent products that can assist restaurant operators and complete tasks on their behalf — joining Toast and Thanx in the growing agentic AI field for food service.
 
Square also launched a restaurant inventory tool in partnership with MarketMan, offering AI-driven ingredient and recipe management directly within Square's platform, aimed at "ingredient-level intelligence" without operators juggling multiple systems.


In distribution infrastructure, 
Amadeus acquired SkyLink — a PhocusWire Hot 25 startup that sits within enterprise chat systems like Microsoft Teams and Slack to enable natural language business travel booking — saying it will expand SkyLink's AI-native conversational solution across airlines, airports, and hospitality.
 
Hong Kong-based DirectBooking and DeepYou have formed a JV to build an AI-native hotel booking platform covering 100,000 hotels over three years, offering natural language interaction, intelligent recommendations, real-time room-status data, dynamic pricing, and demand forecasting.


**So what:** The vendor landscape is bifurcating into MCP-native and MCP-adjacent (i.e., legacy with AI branding). For a PE-backed SaaS company, Deliverect's KFC case study and the Amadeus/SkyLink move represent the acqui-hire and build playbook worth benchmarking against. ## 4. ⚙️ OPERATIONAL AI
### *Back-of-house is leading; measured case studies > vendor claims*

BCG's **AI-First Hotels** report provides the best empirical grounding this week. 
Hotels are seeing RevPAR gains of up to 15% after implementing AI-powered pricing systems that adjust in real time based on demand signals. The Ritz-Carlton San Francisco reported a 20% increase in room-cleaning speed via an AI system optimising housekeeping schedules based on checkout timing, guest preferences, and staffing levels.
 
Meanwhile, 25% of hospitality firms fell into the "AI-scaling" category — meaning they have an AI strategy producing real returns across multiple activities including marketing, revenue management, guest engagement, and property operations.


In restaurants, 
Shake Shack's newly announced Project Catalyst embeds proprietary AI capabilities aimed at real-time operational insights and enterprise-level decision support — with AI positioned as an operating layer across systems rather than a standalone innovation.
 
Loman AI's CEO Christian Wiens reports that voice AI is now "utility-grade infrastructure" for restaurants, with pizza and high-volume takeout categories 12–18 months ahead and already seeing 26%+ phone revenue increases.
 At the IHIF EMEA in Berlin, 
Mews founder Richard Valtr predicted AI could push hotel distribution costs from the current 15–20% down to 5–12%. Room Mate Hotels' Kike Sarasola confirmed AI is "definitely" working for revenue management automation, but warned against trying to solve everything with AI: his company is developing named AI personas for each hotel to keep interactions human.


**So what:** The back-of-house wins (housekeeping, pricing, voice) are the easiest to justify in a board deck. The Ritz-Carlton 20% cleaning speed stat and 15% RevPAR gain from BCG are investor-grade data points. Restaurant operators have measurable, faster cycles — use QSR operator case studies to accelerate buy-in from hotel GMs who are still in pilot mode.

## 5. 👥 PEOPLE & SKILLS
### *Marriott India signals the real staffing story: AI as a retention and productivity tool, not a replacement*


Marriott International's COO for APAC, Neeraj Govil, told Skift that AI is changing how Marriott hires and manages human capital planning, but "it is not about eliminating the job, but about how we're getting the jobs done." Initiatives focus on improving recruitment processes, enabling flexible guest experiences, and adapting HR practices to appeal to younger workers.


The skills gap, however, is structural and wide. 
Just 2.9% of full-time travel and tourism employees are skilled in AI, compared to 21% in the technology and media sector.
 
62% of hospitality respondents cited lack of AI expertise as a barrier, 51% struggled with unclear strategy, and 45% faced integration challenges. Hotels with executive champions who understand the transformation move decisively; those treating AI as an IT problem move slowly, if at all.
 
PhocusWire notes that modern AI training platforms differ from traditional knowledge bases in that they understand intent, adapt content in real time based on role and experience level, and learn which interventions reduce errors and escalations — creating continuous learning environments that improve retention.


**So what:** The talent gap is the least-discussed but most durable constraint. Any advisory mandate should include an AI literacy programme as a deliverable, not an afterthought. The 2.9% vs. 21% AI skills stat is a board-level talking point that reframes AI adoption as a talent acquisition issue, not just a technology procurement issue.

## 6. 🔐 GOVERNANCE & SECURITY
### *Consumer trust is the real constraint on agentic AI — and regulation is starting to formalise it*


Expedia Group's AI Trust Gap data shows that travelers' concerns about AI-driven transactions centre on data privacy, loss of control, and the misuse of personal information — with 40% concerned about poor customer service if something goes wrong after AI has made a purchase.
 
Skift's earlier analysis captured it starkly: "Travelers say they aren't ready to let AI book a trip. The industry is spending billions anyway — and no one has answered the most basic question: who pays when AI agents get it wrong?" Only 2% of leisure travelers currently allow autonomous booking.


On the vendor side, governance is being designed in rather than added later. 
Minor Hotels has embedded OneTrust directly into its platform architecture for privacy and consent management, enabling personalisation at scale while maintaining transparency — framed explicitly as a competitive differentiator, not a compliance checkbox.
 
PhocusWire's assessment: "In 2026, agentic AI in hospitality won't be judged by how fluent or 'smart' it sounds but by how safely it can influence decisions across the business."


**So what:** For a PE-backed SaaS company, governance and trust architecture are now a pricing and retention lever. Hotels that can demonstrate they own and govern their guest data — especially under GDPR and equivalents — will have a defensible moat against OTA AI disintermediation. Embed OneTrust-style consent frameworks into your product roadmap before regulators mandate it.

## 7. 💰 FUNDING & M&A
### *$1B+ into hospitality tech; PMS is the control layer thesis is now investor consensus*


40 global hospitality technology startups raised more than $1 billion between April 2025 and March 2026, according to Abode Worldwide's Hospitality Tech Investment Index 2026. The majority of funding flowed into PMS and AI-driven platforms, reflecting strong investor confidence in foundational technologies that power hotel operations.
 
The biggest raise went to Mews at $300M, with the three largest rounds — Mews, Kindred ($125M), and Limehome (€75M) — landing in a 90-day burst between December 2025 and February 2026, suggesting investors are backing hospitality tech as a category rather than isolated bets.
 
AI-led guest experience platforms were a "standout" investment, with Duve, Chatlyn, Conduit, and Canary Technologies raising a combined $152.6 million — all focused on solving personalised, responsive service in an industry facing labour shortages.


On M&A strategy, 
PwC forecasts that the biggest opportunities in 2026 M&A will centre on "creating connected ecosystems and scaling AI-driven platforms," with corporate acquirers focused on properties that can expand loyalty ecosystems, enhance personalisation capabilities, or deepen cross-channel customer engagement.
 
Private equity remains cautious, contributing just ~35% of disclosed deal value — a sharp drop from nearly 60% in 2024 — with PE deals skewing to luxury resort acquisitions.


**So what:** PMS is the most defensible position in the hospitality tech stack right now. For a PE-backed SaaS player, the consolidation thesis around PMS + AI-led guest experience is institutional-grade, but the compression in average deal size (down ~45%) means investors want tighter proof of operational dependence and recurring revenue before writing checks. Show ARR anchored to daily operator workflows, not feature breadth.
